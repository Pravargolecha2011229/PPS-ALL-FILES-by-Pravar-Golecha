About the Subject

What is PPS?

Personal and Professional Skills (PPS) is a subject designed to help students develop essential life and career skills alongside academic learning. It focuses on building communication skills, critical thinking, leadership abilities, ethical awareness, teamwork, and personal growth. PPS encourages self-reflection, adaptability, and confidence, preparing students not just for examinations, but for real-world challenges. Through projects, activities, and practical experiences, PPS helps individuals grow into responsible, skilled, and future-ready professionals.

Stop Child Labour

Awareness, education, and advocacy for every child's right

Overview

About the Initiative

The "Stop Child Labour" initiative highlights the urgent need to protect children's rights and ensure access to education, safety, and dignity. Child labour remains a serious global issue that deprives children of their childhood, education, and opportunities for a better future. This project was created to spread awareness about the importance of eliminating child labour and promoting equal opportunities for every child.

Understanding the Issue

What Is Child Labour?

Child labour refers to the employment of children in work that:

  • Is mentally, physically, socially, or morally dangerous
  • Interferes with their schooling
  • Exploits their vulnerability

Children engaged in labour are often deprived of proper education, healthcare, emotional well-being, and safe living conditions.

Purpose

Purpose of the Initiative

  • To educate people about the harmful effects of child labour
  • To promote the importance of education for all children
  • To encourage responsible citizenship
  • To highlight the need for ethical and sustainable practices

Key Message

Children belong in schools, not workplaces. Education empowers children with knowledge, confidence, and opportunities. When children are forced into labour, society loses potential innovators, leaders, and contributors. Stopping child labour is not only a legal responsibility but also a moral duty.

Impact

Impact on Personal Growth

Working on this initiative strengthened my social awareness, empathy, sense of responsibility, and understanding of human rights. It helped me realize that even small awareness efforts can contribute to larger social change.

IB Connection

Connection to IB Learner Profile

CaringPrincipledOpen-mindedReflectiveCommunicator
  • Caring — showing empathy and commitment to others' well-being
  • Principled — standing up for justice and fairness
  • Open-minded — understanding social inequalities
  • Reflective — evaluating my role in creating change
  • Communicator — spreading awareness effectively
Conclusion

Final Reflection

The "Stop Child Labour" initiative reinforced the idea that education and equality are fundamental rights for every child. By raising awareness and promoting ethical responsibility, we can contribute to building a society where every child has the opportunity to learn, grow, and succeed. Even small actions can create meaningful impact.

Project 04 / 20

International Business Customs

Cross-cultural workplace analysis through Chanakya's Niti framework

Overview

About the Study

In today's interconnected global economy, international business operates across diverse cultural landscapes where communication styles, workplace etiquette, and negotiation customs determine success or failure. This activity explores how cultural diversity creates opportunities for innovation and collaboration, communication barriers and misunderstandings, and strategic advantages when managed effectively.

Using Chanakya's Niti framework — Saam, Dam, Dand, and Bhed — this study analyzes workplace cultures across five major economies.

Framework

Chanakya's Principles

Saam

Persuasion and communication

Dam

Incentives and rewards

Dand

Punishment and consequences

Bhed

Strategic division or tactical advantage

Country Analysis

Five Business Cultures

Japan

Japan's business culture is deeply rooted in patience, respect, and consensus-building. The workplace environment is highly hierarchical, with strong emphasis on harmony. Collective responsibility is valued over individual recognition. In negotiations, Japanese professionals prioritize relationship-building before discussing business matters. The ringi system of consensus decision-making ensures proposals are carefully reviewed at multiple levels. Silence during negotiations is interpreted as thoughtful contemplation rather than hesitation. Japan strongly reflects Saam through persuasion built on trust, Dam through status recognition, and Dand through the cultural consequence of losing face.

Germany

Germany's business culture is characterized by precision, structure, and legal clarity. The concept of "Ordnung" (order and structure) shapes workplace expectations, where punctuality, discipline, and clear hierarchy are strictly maintained. Professional life is kept separate from personal relationships. Negotiations are data-driven and based on logical persuasion supported by facts and detailed documentation. Germany exemplifies Saam through logical argumentation, Dam through emphasis on return on investment, and Dand through strong institutional and legal enforcement.

Brazil

Brazil presents a contrasting model, emphasizing relationships and flexibility. The workplace culture is warm and relationship-oriented, where personal connections often blend with professional dealings. Negotiations frequently involve informal meetings, social dinners, and extended relationship-building phases before formal agreements are finalized. Under Chanakya's principles, Brazil reflects Saam through expressive and relational persuasion, Dam through personal recognition and mutual benefit, and Bhed through leveraging strong relational networks. In Brazil, trust precedes transaction.

China

China's business culture is built upon Guanxi, a system of networks based on trust, reciprocity, and long-term obligation. The workplace environment is heavily influenced by Confucian values that prioritize harmony, respect, and loyalty. Negotiations tend to involve indirect communication and prolonged relationship cultivation. China demonstrates Saam through diplomatic and indirect persuasion, Dam through reciprocal favors and access to influential networks, and Dand through the potential loss of relationship and trust. In China, relationships function as the true contracts.

United States

The United States represents a business culture defined by directness and results orientation. The workplace is individualistic and merit-based, with fast decision-making and strong emphasis on performance outcomes. Negotiations are straightforward, characterized by direct communication and rapid progression toward closure. Contracts are detailed and serve as the primary mechanism for establishing trust. The United States reflects Saam through clear and concise persuasion, Dam through financial incentives and performance rewards, and Dand through legal enforcement mechanisms. In American business culture, clarity equals credibility.

Reflection

Key Insights

Cultural differences are not barriers but variables shaped by history, values, and social structures. Each system is internally consistent and rational within its own context. The real determinant of success in global business is adaptability — a professional's willingness to understand and adjust to different approaches.

Conclusion

Cultural intelligence becomes a competitive advantage. Organizations that invest in cross-cultural training, flexible communication styles, and respect for local customs consistently outperform rigid, one-standard approaches. The success of international business does not lie in eliminating cultural differences, but in understanding, respecting, and strategically adapting to them. Cultural intelligence is no longer optional — it is essential.

Jonathan Livingston Seagull â€” individuality, perseverance, and self-realization

Reflection

About the Book

Jonathan Livingston Seagull is a philosophical novella that tells the inspiring story of a seagull who dares to be different. Unlike other seagulls that focus only on finding food and surviving, Jonathan is passionate about flying. For him, flight is not merely a means of survival but an art, a science, and a path to self-discovery. His desire to perfect his flying skills sets him apart from his flock and ultimately leads to his exile.

Themes

Core Themes

  • Individuality — daring to be different
  • Perseverance — continuing despite rejection
  • Freedom — breaking from societal expectations
  • Self-realization — discovering one's true potential

Jonathan's journey symbolizes the struggle of anyone who chooses to follow their dreams despite criticism or rejection from society. Even after being cast out, he continues to practice, improve, and push beyond his limits.

Analysis

Literary Perspective

Richard Bach uses simple language yet conveys deep philosophical ideas. The story encourages readers to break free from limiting beliefs and societal expectations. It emphasizes that true success is not measured by material achievement but by personal growth and inner fulfillment. Jonathan's transformation from an outcast to a mentor further highlights the importance of sharing knowledge and uplifting others.

Evaluation

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

Powerful motivational message. Inspires readers to pursue excellence, embrace uniqueness, and view challenges as opportunities for improvement.

Limitations

Some readers may find the narrative abstract or overly idealistic, as it focuses more on philosophy than on complex plot development.

Final Reflection

Jonathan Livingston Seagull is a powerful and uplifting read. It reminds us that limitations are often self-imposed and that courage, discipline, and belief in our abilities can help us rise above mediocrity. The novella leaves readers with a lasting message: the purpose of life is to learn, grow, and help others do the same.

Project 07 / 20

Thinking Skills Activity

Left Brain vs. Right Brain — exploring cognitive diversity

Introduction

About the Activity

The "Left Brain vs. Right Brain" activity explores how different thinking styles influence learning, creativity, and decision-making. This concept — known scientifically as brain lateralization — helps us understand how logical reasoning and creative imagination complement each other. Both hemispheres of the brain work together to shape how we think, learn, and solve problems.

Right Hemisphere

The Right Side — Creativity & Emotion

The right hemisphere of the brain is commonly associated with creativity, imagination, and emotional expression. It supports intuitive thinking and open-ended exploration of ideas.

  • Artistic expression and music
  • Visual and spatial awareness
  • Emotional understanding
  • Imagination and storytelling
  • Creative problem-solving
Left Hemisphere

The Left Side — Logic & Structure

The left hemisphere is associated with analytical thinking, structure, and logic. It focuses on reasoning, language, and step-by-step problem-solving.

  • Logical reasoning
  • Language and grammar
  • Analytical thinking
  • Rational decision-making
  • Understanding rules and structure
  • Critical thinking
Q&A

Key Questions Explored

Are the two sides of the brain actually responsible for different functions?

Yes, research in neuroscience shows that certain cognitive functions are more dominant in one hemisphere — a concept known as lateralization. However, both sides constantly communicate through the corpus callosum. Most tasks require coordination between both hemispheres.

Is one side of our brain dominant over the other?

Neuroscience suggests that no one uses only one side. Everyone relies on both hemispheres for daily activities. Individuals may show preferences in thinking style, but these are tendencies rather than strict divisions.

What are the benefits of describing people as left-brained or right-brained?

  • Increase self-awareness
  • Build confidence in personal abilities
  • Support differentiated learning strategies
  • Encourage appreciation for diverse thinking styles

What problems might this description present?

The main limitation is that it oversimplifies how the brain functions. Labeling individuals strictly as "logical" or "creative" can limit personal growth, create stereotypes, and misrepresent scientific understanding. In reality, abilities such as creativity, reasoning, and emotional intelligence rely on integrated brain activity.

Personal Reflection

My Skills and Strengths

Through this activity, I realized that I use both logical and creative thinking in my daily life. On the analytical side, I am organized, structured, and comfortable working step-by-step through tasks. At the same time, I value creativity and imagination. Balancing both sides of thinking strengthens my adaptability, teamwork, and problem-solving abilities.

Key Takeaway

The most effective thinkers are not purely analytical or purely creative. True intellectual strength lies in the integration of logic and creativity. When analytical reasoning and imaginative thinking work together, individuals can achieve deeper understanding, better decisions, and more innovative solutions.

Project 09 / 20

Gratitude Jars

Emotional awareness and the sustainability of mental well-being

Reflection

About the Activity

As part of our Personal and Professional Skills (PPS) and Service Learning classes, we participated in the Gratitude Glass Jar Activity, which helped us understand emotional awareness and sustainability of mental well-being. In this activity, the glass jar symbolized our mind and thinking processes, while the objects placed inside represented different emotional states we experience in daily life.

Positive Emotions

A Balanced Jar

When we added positive emotions such as happiness, calmness, gratitude, and confidence into the jar, it appeared balanced and visually pleasing. This demonstrated how positive emotions contribute to emotional stability and a healthy mindset. Pleasant experiences and positive thinking play a vital role in maintaining inner peace and improving our ability to learn, collaborate, and perform effectively.

Negative Emotions

An Overcrowded Jar

In contrast, when negative emotions such as stress, anger, pressure, and sadness were added, the jar appeared overcrowded and disorganized. This represented how excessive negative emotions can overwhelm the mind and disturb emotional balance. Through this visual and hands-on experience, we understood how unmanaged emotions can affect decision-making, focus, and overall well-being.

PPS Connection

Emotional Management

The activity emphasized the importance of emotional management and self-reflection, both of which are key components of PPS. It encouraged us to recognize our emotions, understand their impact, and consciously work towards developing positive emotional habits. Maintaining emotional balance is essential not only for personal happiness but also for long-term personal and academic growth.

Overall Takeaway

The Gratitude Jar activity provided a simple yet powerful model to understand how emotional awareness and positivity can enhance our mental resilience, learning capacity, and ability to contribute meaningfully to our community.

Project 14 / 20

Imagination & Mindfulness

Applying mindfulness to navigate conflict, emotions, and personal growth

Personal Reflection

A Real-Life Experience

A few months ago, I had a fight with a good friend that made me feel bad. It started with a small misunderstanding in a text message and became a big, emotional argument. I felt stressed and my thoughts raced — I kept replaying the fight, imagining the worst, and blaming myself.

Usually, I would have reacted quickly: sent an angry message, over-explained myself, or avoided my friend. But this time, I stopped. I remembered mindfulness, something I had been practicing.

The Practice

Applying the Technique

I sat down, closed my eyes, and took deep breaths. I focused on how my body felt — the tension in my jaw, the heat in my eyes, my fast heartbeat. I did not try to change anything. I just noticed. I told myself: this feeling will pass.

Instead of thinking about the fight, I focused on my breath, counting each inhale and exhale slowly. When thoughts popped up, I gently brought my attention back to my breath. After ten minutes, I felt calmer.

The Outcome

How It Helped

With that calm, I saw the situation differently. I realized my friend's reaction came from their own issues, not just because of me. I still felt hurt, but it did not consume me. Later that day, I contacted my friend to listen and honestly share my feelings, without blaming them.

Key Learning

Mindfulness did not solve the fight, but it helped me stay calm and approach it with kindness. It showed me that mindfulness is not about ignoring emotions — it is about allowing emotions without letting them control me. This experience demonstrated the power of pausing before reacting.

The Steps

The Mindfulness Technique

  • Pause — stop before reacting impulsively
  • Breathe — take slow, deep breaths to calm the nervous system
  • Notice — observe physical sensations without judgment
  • Refocus — bring attention back to the breath when thoughts arise
  • Reflect — use the resulting clarity to understand the situation
  • Respond — engage with others from a place of calm and empathy
